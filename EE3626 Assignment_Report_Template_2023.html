1.3.2   Bring Your Own Key (BYOK)

Bring Your Own Key (BYOK) is a popular term related to key management for cloud applications [2]. BYOK allows end users to independently generate, back up, and submit their own encryption keys to the cloud2. Essentially, BYOK loses control of its encryption keys after uploading them to the cloud provider. If the key is lost or an error occurs, the data cannot be decrypted, which may cause a standstill. If the key is stolen, the entire security operation is compromised. If the key is lost or stolen, there is little that can be done since the service provider is initially absolved of responsibility for the key. Organizations need to be vigilant in maintaining backups and subjecting their operations to a high level of security measures [3].

1.3.3   Hold Your Own Key (HYOK)

Hold Your Own Key (HYOK, Hold Your Own Key) is an encryption key management method in which end users retain physical ownership and logical control of their escrowed encryption keys, thereby always owning their keys.

HYOK allows immediate revoking of access by deactivating the key's URL. Data associated with a deactivated key is immediately rendered inaccessible or cryptographically shredded until the key's availability is restored. HYOK is ideal for organizations that must adhere to strict regulations and compliance policies.

Encryption concepts and principles.

Encryption is the process of protecting information or data by using a mathematical model to scramble it in such a way that only parties with the decryption key can access it [4]. Encryption works by encoding "plaintext" into "ciphertext", usually by using a mathematical model of encryption called an algorithm. To decode the data back to plaintext requires a decryption key, a string of numbers, or a cipher also created by an algorithm [5].

2.1      The difference between symmetric encryption and asymmetric encryption

This is a subsection. Symmetric encryption uses a single shared key to encrypt and decrypt data. Asymmetric encryption uses two separate keys: one for encrypting data (the public key) and one for decrypting the data (the private key). Symmetric encryption uses shorter keys (usually 128 or 256 bits), while asymmetric keys are longer (sometimes 2048 bits or longer) [6].

 

Symmetric encryption algorithm [18]:

Advanced Encryption Standard (English: Advanced Encryption Standard, abbreviation: AES), also known as Rijndael encryption method (Dutch pronunciation: [ˈrɛindaːl], sounds like "Rhine doll" in English), is a block adopted by the US federal government. encryption standards. This standard is used to replace the original DES, has been analyzed by many parties and is widely used all over the world. After a five-year selection process, the Advanced Encryption Standard was published by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) in FIPS PUB 197 on November 26, 2001, and became an effective standard on May 26, 2002. Today, the Advanced Encryption Standard is one of the most popular algorithms for symmetric key encryption.

 

The AES encryption process operates on a 4×4-byte matrix, which is also called "state", and its initial value is a plaintext block (the size of an element in the matrix is one of the plaintext blocks). Byte). (Because the Rijndael encryption method supports larger blocks, the "row number" of the matrix can be increased depending on the situation.) When encrypting, each round of AES encryption (except the last round) includes 4 steps:

 

1. AddRoundKey—each byte in the matrix is XORed with the round key; each subkey is generated by the key generation scheme.

2. SubBytes—Through a non-linear replacement function, each byte is replaced with the corresponding byte by means of a lookup table.

3. ShiftRows—Currently shift each row in the matrix.

4. MixColumns—in order to fully mix the operation of each column in the matrix. This step uses a linear transformation to mix the four bytes within each row. The MixColumns step is omitted in the last encryption loop and replaced by another AddRoundKey.

 

AddRoundKey step

In the AddRoundKey step, the bytes in each state are XORed (⊕) with the round key.

In the AddRoundKey step, the round key will be merged with the original matrix. In each encryption cycle, a round key (generated through the Rijndael key generation scheme) will be generated by the master key. The size of this key will be the same as the original matrix, so as to correspond to each XOR (⊕) addition of the bytes.

Figure 2, AddRoundKey step

 

SubBytes step

In the SubBytes step, each byte in the matrix is transformed through an 8-bit S-box. This step provides the non-linear transformation capability of the encryption method.

The S-box is related to the multiplicative inverse element on , known to have good nonlinear properties. To avoid simple algebraic attacks, S-box is constructed by combining multiplicative inverse elements and an invertible affine transformation matrix. In addition, when constructing the S-box, fixed points and anti-fixed points are deliberately avoided, that is, the result of replacing bytes with the S-box will be equivalent to the result of misalignment. The Rijndael S-box entry has a detailed description of the S-box.


Figure 3, SubBytes step

ShiftRows step

ShiftRows describes the row manipulation of a matrix. In this step, each row is rotated left by some offset. In AES (128-bit block size), the first line remains unchanged, and each byte in the second line is rotated one space to the left. Similarly, the leftward cyclic shift offsets of the third row and the fourth row are 2 and 3 respectively. The cyclic shift modes of the 128-bit and 192-bit blocks at this step are the same. After ShiftRows, each vertical column in the matrix is composed of elements in each different column in the input matrix. In the version of the Rijndael algorithm, the offset is slightly different from AES; for a block with a length of 256 bits, the first line remains unchanged, and the offsets of the second, third, and fourth lines are respectively 1-byte, 2-byte, 3-byte. In addition, the ShiftRows operation steps are the same in Rijndael and AES.

In the MixColumns step, the four bytes of each column are combined with each other by a linear transformation. 2.2      The principle and application of hash function

A hash function is a mathematical function that converts a numeric input value into another compressed value. The input to a hash function is of arbitrary length, but the output is always of fixed length. Hash functions are used in many aspects of security to generate digital signatures and verify the integrity of data. They are also used in password storage to protect user passwords.

Hashing is used in network security to help ensure the integrity of data. For example, when a file is downloaded from the Internet, its hash can be checked against the hash stored on the server to ensure that the file has not been altered in transit. [7] Hash functions can encrypt data, generate digital signatures, and create one-way hashes to store passwords securely. [7] Hashing can also be used to verify the authenticity of documents and messages. [7]

3       Presentation Slides…………..…………………[30% of assignment marks]

Understand efficient key management system.

Can key settings be reused if they are secure enough? Is it necessary to change the key regularly? How to recover the lost key? How can two parties who have never met each other conduct key negotiation safely?

The effectiveness of a privacy protection scheme based on cryptography largely depends on whether the key can be managed effectively. Here, we will further analyze the specific scope of key management, the typical risks of each operation link, and the countermeasures.

 

The object of key management is the key itself or the key material used to generate the key (often referred to as the root key). The three main operation links include the use, storage, and negotiation of the key.

 

In view of the differences in capabilities between human users (hereinafter referred to as users) and computer systems, it is necessary to use different technical means and governance means to achieve effective key management. The following will analyze the three types of operation links one by one.

 

Key usage

Key usage refers to the process in which users generate actual keys for different business operations based on the root key. This process not only includes directly using the pre-set key, such as inputting the user password in the user's memory, but also includes using the key after certain transformation.

 

The main risk is unauthorized use caused by key disclosure, which may result in the following consequences:

 

The private data encrypted by this key is leaked. Especially when all historical private data is encrypted with only one key, it is possible for an attacker to obtain the plaintext of all historical private data.

Use this key to authenticate into the system. Not just data, attackers can obtain all the operating privileges of users, for example, maliciously modify system access control parameters, use digital certificates with legal effects to digitally sign unauthorized content, etc.

In response to these risks in the key use link, the core countermeasure is key rotation, that is, a new key is generated at regular intervals.

 

For computer systems, new random keys can generally be easily generated. The new key may not be associated with the old key, and its validity period and the key itself will be stored in a high-security storage medium to minimize the risk of key exposure.

 

However, the above solutions are not very usable for users.

 

It is no longer an easy task for users to generate a secure new key and be able to remember and use it. If the user is further required to memorize multiple ultra-long, random, and unrelated keys, the user is likely to be forced to "work around" and use unsafe means, such as writing all the keys on paper, unprotected in the mobile app.

 

How to let users memorize only one key and realize effective key rotation, the key technology that can be used here is Key Derivation Function (KDF).

 

KDF has two core functions:

Extend a short user password to a key that satisfies the secure key length.

Generate multiple keys that meet the security key length from one root key.

 

A typical KDF, such as the PBKDF2 function in the IETF RFC 2898 standard, can be expressed as follows:

DerivedKey = PBKDF2(PRF, Password, Salt, IterationCount, DerivedKeyLength)

 

Among this:

PRF is a random number generator function, which is responsible for generating a series of random numbers during the operation.

Password is the user password.

Salt is a salt value set to prevent batch brute force attacks, and its function is equivalent to a user-specific random seed.

IterationCount is the number of iterative calculations required when generating a derived key. By deliberately increasing the number of iterative calculations, it is more difficult for an attacker to attack exhaustively.

DerivedKeyLength is the length of the derived key, generally much longer than the user password.

Among the five inputs of the PBKDF2 function, the user only needs to memorize the user password Password, and the other can be calculated by the auxiliary computer system. User passwords can be set according to user preferences without affecting user experience. At the same time, if the user password is long enough, security risks are generally controllable.

 

In addition to PBKDF2, the core of the Hierarchical Deterministic key wallet design in the BIP-32 standard is also KDF. The difference is that BIP-32 provides a unique key derivation rule for the elliptic curve public key cryptography algorithm and realizes the subkey tree expansion and the intermediate node public key escrow expansion.

Figure 6, KDF Technology

 

KDF technically realizes key rotation, and it is necessary to take certain measures in terms of governance to further reduce the risk of key use. Common governance strategies mainly cover two major areas of risk:

 

Minimum key length and complexity: The key length should not be too short to be easily cracked by common dictionary libraries.

Key reuse: It is recommended to change the key regularly, and historical keys cannot be reused. For computer systems, it may be further required to set different keys for different system purposes.

Regarding the first governance strategy, the industry generally has no objection, but there are some different views in recent years on the second suggestion that users need to change the key regularly.

 

In practice, it is often found that, to facilitate memory, many users have adopted unsafe workarounds and selected a group of user passwords with strong associations. For example, the old password used in 2019 is "password2019", and the new password used in 2020 is "password2020". Once the old password is leaked, it is easy to deduce the new password.

 

Conversely, if users are informed that they do not need to change their passwords regularly, users will be psychologically more motivated to set a more complex password. Therefore, implementing a policy of periodically changing user passwords is not necessarily more secure.

 

In addition to the above governance strategies, to control the risk of insiders misusing the key, it is also necessary to assign the control of the key to the hands of relevant personnel who are mutually restrained by multiple functions. Only when all relevant personnel agree to use it can it be normal the technical principles behind it will be mentioned in the next section.

 

key storage

The preservation of the key means that the user saves the key in the storage medium, and in a specific case, restores the previously saved key from the storage medium.

 

The main risk is that the key is leaked or lost due to improper storage. In addition to the consequences mentioned in the previous link, the following additional consequences may be caused:

Private data encrypted by this key cannot be decrypted.

The benefits protected by this key cannot be redeemed.                 

 

For these risks in the key storage link, the core countermeasures are physical isolation and key sharding.

The former means that the environment where the key is kept should be a secure environment isolated from malicious environments. The latter means that the key should not be stored and retrieved as a whole when it is saved but should be fragmented and stored separately by multiple trusted parties. If necessary, multi-site disaster recovery needs to be implemented.

 

For computer systems, secure hardware modules and server rooms with high physical security are common means to achieve physical isolation. If necessary, the device that saves the key can always be kept offline to prevent unexpected unauthorized access.

 

For key sharding, it can be realized by using cryptographic secret sharing algorithm. The most used cryptographic secret sharing algorithm is the Shamir secret sharing algorithm, which was proposed by Israeli cryptographer Adi Shamir in his 1979 paper "How to share a secret".

The core idea of the Shamir secret sharing algorithm is to set the value of the key as a constant parameter in an N-order random polynomial, and then randomly select the coordinates of M points on the random polynomial, and these coordinates are the slices about the key.

Figure3, Sharding of keys

 

These shards have the following properties:

If the total number of shards obtained by the attacker is less than N, the attacker cannot obtain any information about the key.

If the total number M of all possible fragments is greater than N, the key can be effectively recovered after the random polynomial is recovered using the Lagrangian polynomial interpolation algorithm through any N of the fragments.

 

Compared with computer systems, the user's requirement for the former physical isolation may be easier to achieve. Compared with reading the data in the storage medium, it is much more difficult to directly extract the password in the user's memory by technical means without coercion or temptation.

 

However, for the second requirement of key sharding, it is necessary to cooperate with various escrow technologies and use computer-aided means to generate and store high-security key shards. For specific technical classification and comparison, please refer to the previous key escrow related content.

 

No matter which technology is used, generally, the user needs to memorize at least one user password. But like house keys, forgotten user passwords are not uncommon, especially if the number of accounts and the total number of associated passwords is large.

If the service provider provides an effective reset service, the risk of leakage may be lower by resetting the user password through this service than by writing all the user password on paper or in a mobile app.

4.1      Advantages and disadvantages of symmetric encryption, such as AES and DES algorithms

Symmetric encryption algorithms such as AES and DES are widely used in network security. Here are some advantages and disadvantages of symmetric encryption:

advantage:

Symmetric encryption is faster than asymmetric encryption because it uses a single key to encrypt and decrypt data.

Symmetric encryption is less complex than asymmetric encryption because it uses only one key.

shortcoming:

Symmetric encryption is less secure than asymmetric encryption because the same key is used for encryption and decryption.

Symmetric encryption requires a secure method to exchange keys between sender and receiver.

This is a subsection.

Asymmetric encryption algorithms such as RSA and ECC have several advantages:

For example, they are considered very secure and widely used for secure data transmission. [9] They also solve the key sharing problem associated with symmetric encryption schemes by using public key encryption and private key decryption. [10]

 

Asymmetric encryption also has some disadvantages:

4.3     Application and security of hash functions, such as SHA and MD5 algorithms

Hash functions such as SHA and MD5 are used to map arbitrary-length messages to fixed-length message digests. [12] They are often used to check data integrity and identify files, since comparing hash values is easier and faster than comparing the data itself. They are also used for authentication purposes, to store confidential data such as passwords in databases, or for password verification. [13]

 

For cryptographic purposes, SHA (Secure Hash Algorithm) is considered more secure than MD5 (Message Digest Algorithm 5) because it produces longer, more complex hashes. [14] SHA is used in more modern applications and is recommended by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST), while MD5 is considered obsolete and vulnerable. [14]

An overview of the three encryption and key management options:

 

Cloud-based encryption is a method of securing data by encrypting it before storing it in the cloud. There are several options for managing encryption keys used in cloud-based encryption, including Bring Your Own Key (BYOK) and Hold Your Own Key (HYOK).

BYOK is a scenario where customers import (carry) keys from an external storage location into a cloud key management service. [15] Customer generated keys must be stored in Azure Key Vault for BYOK protection. [16]

HYOK enables organizations to secure data in a keyed manner. BYOK hosts RMS keys in an Azure Key Vault HSM, while HYOK lets organizations run their own AD, their own RMS server, and their own HSM for key retention. [17]

 

Advantages, disadvantages and applicable scenarios of encryption and key management

Encryption and key management have several advantages and disadvantages. Encryption can be very secure when using secure algorithms and is relatively fast. It plays a vital role in protecting customer content by encrypting all data at rest and in transit using some of the strongest and most secure encryption protocols available.

 

However, encryption and key management also have some disadvantages. Shared secrets can be a disadvantage and can cause more damage if the key is compromised.

Encryption and key management also have several applicable scenarios. For example, Azure Key Vault can be used to securely store and tightly control access to tokens, passwords, certificates, API keys, and other secrets. It can also be used as a key management solution to create and control the encryption keys used to encrypt data.

 

Development Trends and Challenges of Future Encryption Technology

The trend in encryption technology is to encrypt all private data, especially data that is particularly sensitive or valuable in some way (such as personal or financial data). One of the emerging trends includes the use of quantum-resistant encryption algorithms, such as lattice-based or code-based cryptography, to address the potential threat of quantum computing.

One of the challenges of cryptography is the potential threat of quantum computing, which could render some current encryption algorithms obsolete or vulnerable. Another challenge is that key-based encryption methods may be sufficient today, but they have many weaknesses that reduce their effectiveness as the number and sophistication of data security threats increase. For example, when an encryption key is lost - the data used to encrypt it is effectively lost as well.

Overall, cloud encryption technology is a diverse and growing field that promises to provide more secure and efficient data protection for cloud services soon. In the future, we expect to see more innovations and developments to further enhance the security and reliability of cloud computing.
